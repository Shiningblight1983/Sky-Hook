Estimate Confidence —
Formula Used: Standard LCL (Lifting Condensation Level)
Fahrenheit: H_agl = [(T − T_d) ÷ 4.4] × 1,000 ft  |   Celsius: H_agl = [(T − T_d) ÷ 2.5] × 1,000 ft
Source rate: ~5.4°F/1,000ft dry adiabatic lapse + ~1.0°F/1,000ft dewpoint decrease → net 4.4°F/1,000ft spread closure. Valid primarily for convective cumulus-family clouds under standard tropospheric conditions. Accuracy degrades near fronts, inversions, or marine layers.
Principle: If you observe an object at a measurable elevation angle above the horizon, and you know (or can estimate) the cloud base height, you can establish a lower/upper altitude bound by comparing whether the object appears above or below the cloud deck. With elevation angle and distance-to-reference, basic trigonometry yields estimated altitude. Two observers separated by a known baseline enable true geometric triangulation.

// TWO-OBSERVER BASELINE TRIANGULATION
For highest accuracy: Two observers at a known separation (baseline) both measuring elevation angle to the same object yield altitude via: H = B × tan(α) × tan(β) / (tan(α) + tan(β)) where B is baseline distance, α and β are elevation angles from each observer.

Why a single observer matters:
A single ground observer with a GPS position, a calibrated temperature/dew point reading, and a measured elevation angle can constrain an aerial object's altitude to within a physical bracket. That constraint — above or below the cloud base — is binary and mathematically airtight if the LCL is well-computed. It eliminates hypotheses, not just narrows them. With two coordinated observers, uncertainty collapses further. The SovereignSkies network exists to aggregate these single-observer constraints into statistically meaningful clusters.

Your observation is not anecdote. It is a bounded physical measurement.
Report Philosophy: A scientific field report records what was observed and measured — not what it was. Altitude constraints, angular data, and atmospheric conditions let future analysis discriminate between hypotheses. Speculation is labeled as such. Reproducibility and precision over narrative.

Scientific Basis


    

      Cloud Base Altitude — The Physics


      Unsaturated air rising through the atmosphere cools at the dry adiabatic lapse rate (DALR) of
      approximately 5.4°F per 1,000 feet (9.8°C/km). The dew point decreases more slowly — roughly
      1.0°F per 1,000 feet (1.8°C/km). The spread between ambient temperature and dew point therefore
      closes at approximately 4.4°F per 1,000 feet. The altitude at which spread → 0 is the
      Lifting Condensation Level (LCL) — the theoretical cloud base.
    


    

      LCL Formula (Standard / Aviation Rule of Thumb):

      H_agl(ft) = [(T_F − T_d_F) / 4.4] × 1,000

      H_agl(m) = [(T_C − T_d_C) / 2.5] × 1,247


      For citizen science purposes, the standard formula is sufficient at ±200–500 feet
      under convective conditions.
    


    

      Triangulation Geometry


      Single-observer (range + angle): H_agl = D_horizontal × tan(θ_elevation)

      Physical size: Size = 2 × D_slant × tan(θ_angular / 2) where D_slant = D_horizontal / cos(θ_elevation)

      Two-observer baseline: H = B × [tan(α) × tan(β)] / [tan(α) + tan(β)]
