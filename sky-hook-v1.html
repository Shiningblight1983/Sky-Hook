Scientific Basis


    

      Cloud Base Altitude — The Physics


      Unsaturated air rising through the atmosphere cools at the dry adiabatic lapse rate (DALR) of 
      approximately 5.4°F per 1,000 feet (9.8°C/km). The dew point decreases more slowly — roughly 
      1.0°F per 1,000 feet (1.8°C/km). The spread between ambient temperature and dew point therefore 
      closes at approximately 4.4°F per 1,000 feet. The altitude at which spread → 0 is the 
      Lifting Condensation Level (LCL) — the theoretical cloud base.
    


    

      LCL Formula (Standard / Aviation Rule of Thumb):

      H_agl(ft) = [(T_F − T_d_F) / 4.4] × 1,000

      H_agl(m) = [(T_C − T_d_C) / 2.5] × 1,247


      Bolton (1980) More Precise LCL:

      T_LCL = T / (1 − (T × ln(RH/100) / 2840)) + 55 — gives LCL temperature

      H_LCL(m) = (T − T_LCL) / 9.8 × 1000 (per 1°C/100m dry adiabatic)


      For citizen science purposes, the standard formula is sufficient at ±200–500 feet 
      under convective conditions.
    


    

      Triangulation Geometry


      Single-observer (range + angle):

        H_agl = D_horizontal × tan(θ_elevation)


      Physical size from angular diameter:

        Size = 2 × D_slant × tan(θ_angular / 2)

        D_slant = D_horizontal / cos(θ_elevation)


      Two-observer baseline (most accurate, no range needed):

        H = B × [tan(α) × tan(β)] / [tan(α) + tan(β)]

        where B = baseline distance, α and β = elevation angles from each observer


      The baseline method is mathematically independent of range estimation and 
      should be preferred when two observers can coordinate.
    


    

      Cloud Constraint Method (Unique to This Tool)


      A cloud deck at a known altitude creates an observable upper or lower bound:

      • Object clearly below cloud base → altitude is LESS THAN computed LCL

      • Object apparently above or in clouds → altitude is GREATER THAN computed LCL

      • Object entered / exited cloud edge → altitude approximately EQUALS LCL ± uncertainty


      This constraint is often the most observationally accessible for untrained observers 
      and should always be recorded, as it provides a constraint independent of 
      angle/range estimation errors.

      Cell Tower Generation Heights — Summary


      5G small cell (mmWave/dense urban): ~20–50 ft on utility poles and streetlights

      5G/4G macro tower (mid-range): 50–200 ft — typical suburban/urban deployment

      3G/4G macro tower (rural long-range): 200–400 ft — FAA lit, covers 25–40 miles

      Rooftop antenna installations: 2–30 ft above host structure (not standalone height)


      Key fact: The 199 ft ceiling is an engineering/economic choice, not a technical limit. 
      Tower owners stop just below 200 ft to avoid mandatory FAA aviation lighting maintenance costs. 
      Most of what you see driving through suburban areas is in the 120–199 ft range.
    


    

      What This Tool Cannot Do


      • Identify what was observed — that is not science, that is hypothesis

      • Compensate for optical illusions, perspective effects, or unusual atmospheric refraction

      • Verify observer accuracy or bias

      • Replace calibrated instruments (radar, lidar, theodolite)


      What it CAN do: take a raw visual observation and extract the maximum 
      physically constrained information from it, documented in a format 
      that other researchers can evaluate and reproduce.
